When to Replace

How Do You Know When It's Time to Replace Your AC System?

Your system is 12–15 years old or older

The average lifespan of a central AC system in Southern California is 12–17 years with regular annual maintenance - and 8–10 years without. A system that is 12 years or older is approaching the end of its cost-effective service life, regardless of whether it is currently running. At this age, the cost of repairs - particularly compressor or coil failures - often exceeds 50% of the cost of a new system.

Your energy bills have increased significantly

A central AC system that is losing efficiency draws more electricity to produce the same cooling output. If your SCE or LADWP bill has increased noticeably over the past 1–2 summers without a change in usage habits or rate structure, declining AC efficiency is a likely contributor. Modern high-efficiency systems with SEER2 ratings of 16–20+ can reduce cooling energy consumption by 20–40% compared to a 12-year-old system.

Your system uses R-22 refrigerant

R-22 - also known as Freon - was phased out of production in 2020 under EPA regulations. R-22 is no longer manufactured in the United States and existing supplies are finite and increasingly expensive. A system that uses R-22 and develops a refrigerant leak faces repair costs significantly higher than a current-refrigerant system - and an R-22 system cannot be converted to the current refrigerant standard. Replacement with a current-refrigerant system is the only long-term solution.

Your system has required multiple repairs in the past 2–3 years

A pattern of recurring repairs - capacitor this year, contactor last year, motor the year before - is a reliable indicator that a system is entering component failure cascade. Each repair addresses one failing part while leaving all adjacent aging components in place. When the repair history over 2–3 years approaches $1,000–$1,500, replacement economics typically favor a new system.

System Sizing

How Do You Size an AC System Correctly for a Los Angeles Home?

Correct system sizing is the most important technical decision in an AC installation - and the one most commonly done incorrectly by contractors who use rules of thumb rather than proper calculation methods.

What is a Manual J load calculation?

A Manual J load calculation is the ACCA-approved industry standard for determining the correct AC system size for a specific home. Manual J accounts for the home's total square footage, ceiling height, insulation levels, window area and solar orientation, local climate data - using TMY weather data for the specific Los Angeles microclimate - number of occupants, and heat-generating appliances. The result is a precise cooling load in BTUs per hour - the number the system must be capable of removing to maintain comfort at peak conditions.

Why does correct sizing matter?

An undersized system runs continuously at peak conditions but cannot reach the thermostat setpoint - leaving your home uncomfortable on the hottest days of the year. An oversized system - the more common installation error - short cycles, turning on and off every few minutes rather than completing full cooling cycles. Short cycling prevents adequate humidity removal, creates temperature swings, wastes electricity, and causes premature compressor wear. According to ACCA, up to 50% of residential HVAC systems in the United States are incorrectly sized - the majority being oversized.

AIRONE sizes every installation with Manual J - not guesswork. Call (323) 471-1037 →

What does AIRONE do differently for system sizing?

Every AIRONE installation begins with a Manual J load calculation using ACCA-approved software and actual measurements from your home. The calculation result determines the system size recommendation - not the size of the system that was previously installed, which may itself have been incorrectly sized. AIRONE does not size by rule of thumb and does not replace like-for-like without verifying the prior system was correctly sized.

What size AC system does a Los Angeles home typically need?

While Manual J is the correct sizing method, these ranges provide a general orientation. Most 1,000–1,500 sq ft LA County homes require a 2–2.5 ton system. Most 1,500–2,000 sq ft homes require a 2.5–3 ton system. Most 2,000–2,500 sq ft homes require a 3–3.5 ton system. Most 2,500–3,500 sq ft homes require a 3.5–5 ton system. Homes with high solar gain, poor insulation, or high ceilings may require larger systems than these ranges suggest. Homes with excellent insulation, low-e windows, or significant shade may require smaller systems. These are orientation guidelines only - not substitutes for a Manual J calculation.

Title 24 & Permits

Does AC Installation in Los Angeles Require a Permit?

Yes - and this is one of the most important questions to ask any contractor before signing an installation agreement.

Is a permit required for AC installation in California?

Yes. Under California Building Code, a permit is required for new AC installation and for AC replacement in most cities and counties across Los Angeles County and Orange County. The permit triggers a building inspection that verifies the installation meets California Building Code requirements and Title 24 energy efficiency standards - which govern insulation levels, duct sealing, and equipment efficiency minimums for all new HVAC installations in California.

What is Title 24 and does it affect AC installation in Los Angeles?

Title 24 is California's Building Energy Efficiency Standards - the most stringent residential energy code in the United States, updated January 1, 2026. Title 24 requires that new AC installations in California meet minimum SEER2 efficiency ratings, that all ductwork be tested for leakage, that thermostats meet programmability requirements, and that the installation be verified by a licensed contractor. A Title 24-compliant installation requires documentation - AIRONE provides all required compliance documentation as part of every installation package.

What happens if AC is installed without a permit in Los Angeles?

An unpermitted AC installation in California creates three specific risks. First, homeowner's insurance may deny claims related to a fire or property damage event if an uninspected installation is found to be the cause. Second, unpermitted work creates disclosure obligations in a property sale and may require remediation before closing. Third, some manufacturer warranties are void if the installation was not permitted and inspected. AIRONE includes permit filing and inspection coordination in every installation - this is not optional and is not an upsell.

Does AIRONE handle the entire permit process?

Yes - AIRONE files the permit application with your city or county building department, pays the permit fees as part of the quoted installation cost, schedules the building inspection, and is present during the inspection. You do not need to contact the building department, take additional time off, or manage any part of the permit process. In LA County, Title 24 compliance also requires a HERS (Home Energy Rating System) inspection - performed by an independent certified rater, separate from the building department inspection. Efficiency & Rebates

What SEER2 Rating Should I Choose for a New AC System in Los Angeles?

SEER2 - Seasonal Energy Efficiency Ratio 2 - measures how efficiently an AC system converts electricity into cooling output over an entire season. Higher SEER2 = lower operating cost. Higher SEER2 also means higher upfront equipment cost. Here is how to choose the right efficiency tier for a Southern California home.

Minimum efficiency - 14–15 SEER2

Lowest upfront cost. Highest long-term operating cost.

The current federal minimum efficiency standard for AC systems installed in California. A 14–15 SEER2 system has the lowest upfront cost but the highest long-term operating cost.

This tier is right for you if: your budget is the primary constraint, you plan to sell the home within 5 years, or you are replacing a system in a rental property where long-term energy savings accrue to a tenant.
Mid efficiency - 16–18 SEER2

The best value tier for most Southern California homeowners.

A 16–18 SEER2 system costs $500–$1,500 more than a minimum efficiency system but typically pays back the efficiency premium in 3–5 years in SoCal's long cooling season. Most two-stage systems fall in this range.

This tier is right for you if: you plan to stay in the home 5+ years, you want meaningful energy savings without the premium cost of variable-speed equipment, or you want SCE or LADWP rebate eligibility.
High efficiency - 19–22 SEER2

Variable-speed systems that modulate output based on real-time demand.

Variable-speed systems run at lower capacity most of the time and ramp up only during peak heat. They provide superior humidity control, quieter operation, and more precise temperature maintenance than single-stage or two-stage systems.

This tier is right for you if: you plan to stay in the home 7+ years, you prioritize comfort and humidity control as much as energy savings, or your home has rooms that are consistently harder to cool.
Premium efficiency - 23+ SEER2

The highest efficiency residential AC systems available.

Carrier Infinity 26, Lennox XC25, and comparable units. These systems have the highest upfront cost but the lowest operating cost and typically qualify for the maximum utility rebate tier.

This tier is right for you if: you have a long planning horizon, high electricity usage, and want maximum long-term return on your installation investment.
